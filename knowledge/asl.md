American Sign Language (ASL)

Overview

American Sign Language (ASL) is a complete, natural language that uses
hand shapes, facial expressions, and body movements to convey meaning. It
is the primary language of many Deaf and hard-of-hearing communities in
the United States and parts of Canada. ASL has its own grammar and
sentence structure, which is distinct from spoken or written English.

History

ASL emerged in the early 19th century, heavily influenced by French Sign
Language (LSF) after Laurent Clerc, a Deaf educator from France, co-founded
the American School for the Deaf in Hartford, Connecticut, in 1817 with
Thomas Hopkins Gallaudet. Over time, ASL evolved independently, blending
elements of LSF with signs already used by Deaf communities in the U.S.

Grammar and Structure


ASL follows a Topic-Comment structure rather than the Subject-Verb-Object
order common in English.
Facial expressions and eyebrow movements are grammatical markers, not
just emotional cues -- for example, raised eyebrows can indicate a
yes/no question.
Classifiers (handshapes representing categories of objects) are used to
describe size, shape, and movement.


The ASL Alphabet (Fingerspelling)

Fingerspelling uses a distinct handshape for each letter of the English
alphabet and is used primarily for proper nouns, technical terms, or words
that do not have an established sign. The one-handed ASL manual alphabet
includes 26 handshapes, one for each letter A through Z. Letters such as
"J" and "Z" involve a small movement rather than a static handshape.

The Sign for "Thank You"

The ASL sign for "Thank You" is made by touching the fingertips of a flat
hand to the chin, then moving the hand forward and slightly down toward
the person being thanked -- symbolically "sending" the gratitude outward.

Common Everyday Signs


Hello: A flat hand near the forehead, moving outward and away, similar
to a small salute.
Please: A flat hand rubbed in a circular motion on the chest.
Sorry: A closed fist rubbed in a circular motion on the chest.
Yes: A closed fist nodding up and down like a head nodding "yes."
No: The index and middle fingers tapping the thumb, similar to a
mouth opening and closing while saying "no."


ASL in Education

Many Deaf schools and mainstream classrooms with Deaf students use ASL as
the primary language of instruction, often paired with English literacy
instruction (a bilingual-bicultural, or "Bi-Bi," approach). Interpreters,
captioning, and ASL courses are also increasingly common in mainstream
K-12 and university settings to support inclusive education.
