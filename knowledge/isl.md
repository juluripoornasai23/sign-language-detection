Indian Sign Language (ISL)

Overview

Indian Sign Language (ISL) is the primary sign language used by Deaf
communities across India. Unlike ASL, ISL commonly uses two-handed
handshapes for fingerspelling and many everyday signs, reflecting its
distinct linguistic tradition.

Recognition and Standardization

For many years ISL lacked formal government recognition and standardized
teaching materials, leading to significant regional variation. The Indian
Sign Language Research and Training Centre (ISLRTC), established by the
Government of India, has worked to document, standardize, and promote ISL
through dictionaries, teacher training, and curriculum development.

Grammar and Structure


ISL, like ASL, is a visual-spatial language with its own grammar
independent of any spoken Indian language.
It typically follows a Subject-Object-Verb (SOV) word order, similar to
many spoken Indian languages such as Hindi.
Non-manual markers (facial expressions, head tilts, and body posture)
carry grammatical meaning, such as distinguishing questions from
statements.


The ISL Alphabet (Fingerspelling)

ISL fingerspelling is predominantly two-handed, meaning most letters are
formed using both hands together, in contrast to the one-handed ASL
alphabet. This is one of the most noticeable differences between the two
languages for newcomers.

ASL and ISL are mutually unintelligible languages -- a fluent ASL signer
cannot automatically understand ISL, and vice versa, just as a French
speaker cannot automatically understand Hindi.

ISL in Education

India has increasingly promoted ISL in education through initiatives such
as including ISL as an optional subject in schools and training ISL
interpreters for classrooms. The National Education Policy 2020 explicitly
recognized ISL and encouraged its standardization and use across the
country to improve access to education for Deaf students.
